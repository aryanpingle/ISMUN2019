What is the WHO?

While the world fixates at the pandemonium of nuclear treaties, economic warfare, climate change, political gimmicks, and natural calamities, the WHO works to provide solutions to the problems that have haunted mankind since its very advent: disease and health.

The Committee Agenda

Creating international regulations on the usage of gene editing in humans.

Summary

Mutation, eugenics, and genetic engineering have appeared in the genre of science fiction since the 19th century. The idea of tinkering with the genes we pass to our children, which was once the theme of many a book, despite its technological challenges, is now a conceivable reality.

Genetics, the two-century-young science has progressed very rapidly. The CRISPR/Cas9 technique is so effective at cutting and adding genes that researchers all over the world have adopted it in their labs. According to a September 2016 report by the Nuffield Council on Bioethics, in the future it may be possible to enhance people with genes from other organisms or wholly synthetic genes.

The changes made in an individual’s somatic cells die with them. However, editing genes in the germline would affect their future child’s own egg or sperm as these changes are heritable. Due to the possibility of off-target effects and mosaicism post-editing, safety is of paramount importance.

The primary concern of some bioethicists and researchers is that any genome editing, even for therapeutic uses, will ultimately lead to using it for enhancement purposes, which is a topic of controversy. Others feel that concerns about enhancement should be managed through policy and regulation and that once proved to be effective and safe, gene editing should be allowed to cure genetic disorders.

The agenda will be discussed in a manner in which technical, monetary, as well as ethical aspects of genomic editing, are considered thoroughly. Measures for the articulation of international regulations prevent misuse of the genomic modification technology will be the crux of the discussion and debate.

What must the UN do to prevent illegal genetic editing? How do we draw the line between treatment and enhancement? What steps must be taken to ensure the application of these technologies is responsible, safe and beneficial?

Find the answers in WHO 2019.
